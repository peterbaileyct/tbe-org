Bristol couple finds success with gymnastics business

Originally found at https://www.bristoledition.org/?p=15226 Published on 2022-07-25 13:24:27

By Anna Bedell

Dreams do come true. That is what two Bristol residents, Wesley “Wes” Norris and his wife Lyndsie, are finding.  

The dream goes back to CATS Gymnastics established in Cheshire in the mid-1980s. The Norrises, introduced to CATS in the 1990s, purchased the highly successful business in mid-2019 and have continued a tradition that now includes their daughter.

Norris, 51, worked at the gym for three years the mid-90s while at college. After graduating, he joined the United States Coast Guard and moved to Florida.

“I came back to CATS for another three years in the early 2000s,” said Norris.

In 2017, the Norrises returned to CATS Gymnastics, never forgetting their vision of one day owning the gym.

“We bought the gym in August of 2019,” said Norris.

In February 2020, a flood forced the gym to close its doors for two weeks.

After reopening its doors for only three weeks, the gym was forced to close for another three months because of the pandemic.

By the summer of 2020, the gym reopened once again, and the business has been thriving ever since, according Norris.

In November 2021, CATS expanded by taking over the space next door, increasing the gym space by about 40 percent, he said.  

“We didn’t compete last season because of Covid,” said Norris. “This season, we had great success with some levels winning every meet they went to as a team.”

With multiple State Champions, the CATS Xcel Bronze team won the State Championship team title.

“None of them having ever competed before,” said Norris.

CATS has about 65 kids on their competitive team and close to 400 in the overall program, according to Norris.

Over the years, Norris said, the gym has more than 75 athletes who received scholarships, while also producing several National Team members.

Norris said the recreational class program grows every session. Classes fill up so quickly that parents actually set their alarms, he said, often staying up until midnight to sign-up for classes, as soon as they become available.

For the Norrises, it has become a family business to be proud of.

“My daughter Everley, (she’s eight) has been a gym rat since about 18-months-old, many times in the gym, five-six days a week, “ said Norris. “Because of the hectic gym school and Kindergarten being full days, we decided to home school her right before the pandemic hit.”

According to Norris, the soon-to-be third grader had an incredible season, placing first in all but one of her meets this season.

“In the world of gymnastics, most eight-year-olds are new to gymnastics, just starting recreational classes,” said Norris. “At age eight, Everley is performing skills that many teenagers are just beginning to learn.”

He said his daughter is on a path towards college gymnastics if she chooses to do so.

“She actually worked out with the Southern Connecticut State University gymnastics team last year, garnering quite a few cheers and comments from the college gymnasts,” he said.

The sport of gymnastics has a reputation for being hard on young girls, especially at such a high level of competitive athleticism, he said.

“Lyndsie and I decided when we took over, that our philosophy would be that they are kids first,” said Norris.  “We don’t yell and scream at them.”

The Norrises have high expectations of their athletes; they believe in achievement through positive reinforcement.

“If you look at our success in the last three years, you can see that it works,” he said.

With five years left on their lease, Norris said if they continue to grow as they have, they will need to look for more space.

For the foreseeable future, CATS will stay in Cheshire.

“We have established roots in the town and one of our major goals is to be a large part of the community,” said Norris. “We have participated in parades, fundraisers and a walk for MS since taking ownership.”  

For more information, the public can visit the CATS gymnastics website.
