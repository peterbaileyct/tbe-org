Sen. Martin holds public information meeting regarding Covanta permit

Originally found at https://www.bristoledition.org/?p=9568 Published on 2024-11-20 09:23:07

By Rit Carter

Last Tuesday night at Plymouth Town Hall, 20 people, mostly Bristol residents, attended an information meeting regarding Covanta’s application to burn medical waste at its trash-to-energy plant on Enterprise Dr.

State Senator Henri Martin of the 31st district organized the meeting due to health concerns expressed by his constituents. Dr. Paul Gilman, Chief Sustainability Officer and Vice President for Covanta, attended and provided information and answered questions from those in attendance. In July, via Zoom, a similar meeting took place.

Sen. Martin made introductory remarks before yielding to questions and comments. Former state representative candidate Dave Rackliffe voiced his concerns.

“The permit application is very complete, very thorough,” Rackcliffe said. “But as you read through it, some questions really jumped out at you. A lot of it has to do for me with the tonnage and the amount of waste that was going to be burned in Bristol.”

Rackcliffe said he felt it was unacceptable only to advise the public through city council meetings, “To say that that was adequate communication to the general population, I think this is erroneous, and they paid the price.”

It is worth noting that in the fall of 2018, Covanta went before at least ten different boards and commission to fulfill their environmental justice requirements for the permit and keep the boards, commissions and public in the loop. Additionally, Cheryl Thibeault, now a City Councilwoman, once worked for Covanta and was on the Board of Finance when the host fee agreement came through boards and commissions.

The other piece that Bristol was involved with concerned modifying the host community fee. The previous administration negotiated an increase in what Covanta paid the city based on the host community status, upwards of $300,000. The agreement stipulated that the city would not sanction anatomical or fetal waste.

Covanta’s permit seeks to bring medical waste to the Bristol facility from seven other states, and they believe Bristol is the best suited to accomplish this. “We look for a good operating facility and a good location where there is demand for the service. And the Bristol facility is a good location and a good facility that runs well,” Gilman said.

Dr. Gilman also addressed the company’s 2015 OSHA citation for workplace safety and health standards violations, which resulted in an $80,000 fine. “The safety violations were largely attributable to the electrical systems in the facility. A number of electrical system modifications had been done and were not done properly.”

The biomedical waste coming to Bristol will be generated from clinics, labs, and hospitals. Sen. Martin read a list of allowable materials to be burned in the incinerator, which includes: gauze, beddings, gowns, dressings, needles, syringes, scalpels, IV bags, tubing, catheters, vials, residual medicines, tissue samples on slides, personal protective equipment, curtains, gloves, residual vaccines, and vials.

He also noted the inappropriate materials, those not to be included under the plan: anatomical waste, cadavers, human fetal tissues, bulk therapeutic waste, pharmaceutical waste, and anything that’s radioactive material.

In 2014, Marion County Commissioners in Portland, Ore., ordered a waste-to-energy facility to stop receiving medical waste from British Columbia when they discovered that they might have been burning fetal tissue, cancerous tissue, and amputated limbs to generate power.

Covanta won the New Jersey Business and Industry Association’s (NJBIA) Excellence for Community Service from the New Jersey Business and Industry Association in 2019. The NJBIA is an annual award that acknowledges outstanding businesses and their leaders who have improved New Jersey’s economic climate and quality of life.

Despite invites to DEEP and Bristol municipal leaders, the only Bristol government representative from the state or municipal level in attendance was Sen. Martin. (Bristol had the Board of Finance Meeting and Diversity Council meetings the same evening.) The state senator remarked that this is not just a Bristol issue but a regional one. Additional information sessions are scheduled for Bristol, Plainville, Wolcott, and Southington in the coming months. 

Trash-to-Energy Timeline:

  • 1984 May: Bristol seeks bids on Trash-to-Energy Plant
  • 1985 May: Bristol approves a 25-year deal with Ogden Martin
  • 1985 August: The Connecticut Siting Council approves permit (5-0) for energy plant
  • 1988 March: Ash from trash plant dumped at the city landfill. Despite being found to be high in lead and cadmium, DEP said it would probably allow the ash to be discarded for another two years.
  • 1990 February: Ogden Martin top taxpayer in Bristol.
  • 1991 February: Due to poor attendance, a citizen watchdog group on trash plant disbands.
  • 1991 November: Residents vote 6,254 to 5,395 rejecting a third burner.
  • 1991 November: The town of Avon announces it will ship 70 tons of waste a month to Bristol.
  • 1996 December: Denial of trash plant expansion fought.
  • 2001 March: Ogden Energy Group becomes Covanta.
  • 2011 March: Due to a boiler explosion, two employees were injured.
  • 2015 February: OSHA levels $80,000 fine for inadequate safeguards.
  • 2018 March: Bristol Development Authority Meeting, Covanta Special Permit Modification Request: Mayor Zoppo-Sassu said Covanta wants to change its business model. She explained how it works, the advantage to Covanta and to Bristol. The mayor then answered questions.
  • 2019 February: Public information meeting held in the council chambers regarding Covanta’s medical waste conversion permit
